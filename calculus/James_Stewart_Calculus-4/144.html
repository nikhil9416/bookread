In this section we discuss second-degree equations such as x 2 1 y 2 - 1 y - x 2 1 1 x 2 9 1 y 2 4 - 1 x 2 2 y 2 - 1 which represent a circle, a parabola, an ellipse, and a hyperbola, respectively. The graph of such an equation in x and y is the set of all points s x, yd that satisfy the equation; it gives a visual representation of the equation. Conversely, given a curve in the xy-plane, we may have to ind an equation that represents it, that is, an equation satisied by the coordinates of the points on the curve and by no other point. This is the other half of the basic principle of analytic geometry as formulated by Descartes and Fermat. The idea is that if a geometric curve can be represented by an algebraic equation, then the rules of algebra can be used to analyze the geometric problem.
