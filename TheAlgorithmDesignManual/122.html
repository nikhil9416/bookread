Figure 4.2(l) presents a min-heap ordered tree of red-letter years in American history (kudos to you if you can recall what happened each year). The most natural implementation of this binary tree would store each key in a node with pointers to its two children. As with binary search trees, the memory used by the pointers can easily outweigh the size of keys, which is the data we are really interested in. The heap is a slick data structure that enables us to represent binary trees without using any pointers. We will store data as an array of keys, and use the position of the keys to implicitly satisfy the role of the pointers. We will store the root of the tree in the ﬁrst position of the array, and its left and right children in the second and third positions, respectively. In general, we will store the 2 l keys of the lth level of a complete binary tree from left-to-right in positions 2 l−1 to 2 l − 1, as shown in Figure 4.2(r).
